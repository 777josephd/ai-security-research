# AI Hallucination and Misinformation

A colleague needs a Cybersecurity market brief covering 4 key areas:
- Market size
- AI adoption
- EU regulatory impact
- Vendor market share

Accuracy is critical.

<img width="1600" height="995" alt="ss26" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/e2ebac0c-5a61-4a0b-ab1c-652da6911c35" />

Before drafting the brief, another colleague emails about issues related to AI-generated research encountered the previous week.  
The AI cited a security report that **does not exist**. The numbers and information looked real, but were fabricated.  
Their advice: cross-reference AI statistics against independent sources.

AI models present fabricated numbers with the same confidence and precision as verified data. Metrics may appear authoritative, making them easy to accept without question.

<img width="1600" height="995" alt="ss27" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/392015c5-1706-4216-8ddc-a981e0611760" />

This behavior is called **confabulation**. The AI is not retrieving the wrong information; it is generating plausible text that has no basis in reality.

Partial truths are the hardest AI errors to catch. The underlying topic is real, the trend direction is correct, but the specific numbers are inflated. This type of error can pass a casual review because it "feels" right.

Out of the 4 claims we were interested in, only one was fully accurate.  
One was entirely fabricated.  
One cited a fictional source.  
One exaggerated real data.

Without cross-referencing, all 4 would have made it into critical documentation.

**Key Takeaways**:

**Before including AI output in deliverables:**

- Cross-reference every statistic against the original primary source - not just another AI tool
- Search for cited reports in the publisher's actual catalog to confirm they exist
- Watch for numbers that seem dramatically higher or lower than industry consensus
- Treat AI output as a first draft to be verified, never as a final product

**If you discover AI-generated misinformation:**

- Do not distribute the content - even partially accurate claims can be misleading
- Inform your team so they can apply the same verification to their own AI-assisted work
- Document which claims were wrong and what the verified figures are

**Going forward:**

- Use AI for structuring analysis and identifying relevant topics, not as a primary data source
- Establish a verification step in your workflow before any AI-assisted content reaches clients
- Remember that AI confabulation (fabricated sources) is harder to catch than wrong numbers - always verify citations
